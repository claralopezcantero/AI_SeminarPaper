# AI_SeminarPaper
This paper examines whether the redistribution mechanisms proposed by Koster et al. (2025) [8]
remain equally effective when applied to populations with real behavioral heterogeneity rather than
simulated agents. Using experimental data from public goods games (362 subjects), we identify
behavioral clusters and train LSTM models that act as behavioral clones. These models are used
to simulate repeated interactions under three redistribution mechanisms: Equal, Proportional, and
Interpolating. Our results partially replicate the main findings of Koster et al. [8], as the inter-
polating mechanism continues to provide a balanced trade-off between efficiency and inequality.
However, its advantage in efficiency over the proportional mechanism is reduced and no longer
statistically significant. Furthermore, we show that the performance of these mechanisms strongly
depends on group composition, with differences becoming particularly pronounced in heteroge-
neous and asymmetric groups. In general, our findings highlight that behavioral heterogeneity is a
key factor in understanding the effectiveness of redistribution mechanisms and suggest that their
